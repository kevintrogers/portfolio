As a boy, I loved board games. Whenever someone in my family brought a new game home, I always wanted to be the first one to read the rules. I knew that the better I understood the rules, The better prepared I would be to develop an effective strategy. It also gave me the opportunity to explain the rules and to change them for my younger siblings so that they could enjoy the games more.

I still enjoy board games, but I wrote about this because I find that web development follows similar logic. Learning the rules (languages, frameworks, methods, tools, etc.), allows one to develop better strategies for increasing workflow and reducing bugs. As I have grown as developer, I also really enjoy explaining and discussing concpets with other developers. I am also very grateful to the other developers in my workplace, in meetups, and online who have done the same for me.

The first site I built was a WordPress site that I built for a company out of desperation. The people we had hired to do the work weren't doing. I got drafted, in a sense, to make the website without any experience. A few Google searches later, I had started building my a Word Press site, and it felt like magic. It also looked very amatuerish. I did a google search on HTML/CSS and went on Amazon and bought a book and went through it. My site now looked much more unique. Still not great, but I was going down the right path.

I was living in Los Angeles. My commute home was an hour and fifteen minutes on a good day. I would come home, make dinner for my wife and myself before she headed out to work, feed my young son, bathe him, read to him, sing to him, and put him to bed. Then I would practice coding until 10:00p.m. and go to sleep. I would wake up at 4:30, work out and code until 8:30 and then start work. I did this for a year and loved every minute of it.

At first, I took advice from everybody and anybody. Advice included:

  • Take a Java class and you'll be able to find a job without much of a problems.
  • Word Press isn't worth learning.
  • Ruby is the hottest skill on the planet.
  • You don't really need to learn HTML. If you need to know something just google it.
Being a noob, I thought all of this advice was gold. And I was really enjoying learning new things. After a while, I realized, I hadn't learned enoguh to build real applications. I took more classes. I learned about date structures and algorithms. I used online tutorials and videos. I built some applications from scratch. They looked bad. I started to learn about design, typography, Responsive Design. I really wanted to understand why some sites looked really slick wile others looked cluttered.
